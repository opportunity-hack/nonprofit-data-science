We in the YWCA have a special reason to celebrate, for our own YWCA leader was one of those pilots: Peg Calhoun.¬†
Peg Calhoun served on the Board of Directors of many YWCAs during her career: Long Beach and Mid-Peninsula (Palo Alto) in California; the National Board of the YWCA of the USA during the 1950‚Äôs and 1960‚Äôs; the YWCA of India; and on the Executive Committee of the World YWCA. She and Cal helped Margaret build a camp for women in Zimbabwe for the YWCA.
How did Peg become a pilot? When World War II broke out, she and Cal spoke of what they should do. Cal could not join the service because of a bad back. They decided Peg should be the one to serve.
So, she took flying lessons and even practiced take offs and landings at the San Francisco airport! And she became a pilot in the WASPs‚Äîthe Women‚Äôs Air Force Service Pilots. After the war, Peg began her many years of volunteer work with the YWCA until retiring¬†in Claremont, C.A., where she continued to enjoy the friendship of Margaret Hathaway and Elizabeth Palmer, former General Secretary of the World YWCA, as well as many other YWCA retirees at Pilgrim Place.¬†She was able to attend several special reunions with her flying friends during her lifetime before passing away.
Vibrant, positive, enthusiastic, humorous, full of interesting tales, and a devotee of the YWCA ‚Äî¬†and¬†a good pilot. We will salute you, Peg, as the float rolls by and recognizes the women heroes of the past.
